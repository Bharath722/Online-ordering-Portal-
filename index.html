SMART BANKING - An Introduction

The book explains how with the help of technology banks are providing innovative services by managing wide network of branches and delivery channels. It focuses on key banking activities and IT applications driving the banking processes.

The book describes the inter-connection between the Core Banking Solution, delivery channels and the networking platforms facilitating online transactions between banks, customers and the business entities. Readers will find the book helpful in addressing questions like:

  • How does Anywhere Anytime Banking work?
  • What is Core Banking Solution?
  • How do ATMs function?
  • How are payments effected with Debit/Credit Card?
  • How do instant funds transfer take place?
  • What are Internet Banking, Mobile Banking and Mobile Wallet?
  • What are ‘Aadhaar’ enabled services?
  • How do payments relating to stock market, bills, taxes and e-commerce takes place?

The book also gives overview of:

  • Treasury, Securities Trading and Foreign Exchange Trading Systems
  • Wealth Management, Cash Management and Customer Relationship Management Solutions
  • Loan Origination System
  • Anti Money Laundering and Asset Liability Management Solutions
  • Financial Communication Networks
  • Key RBI Initiatives and emerging trends.

The book will help students of banking, IT professionals engaged in banking applications, bankers and bank customers in scaling up the understanding about technology driven banking.

AN OVERVIEW

Banks deal with individuals, business entities, public enterprises, government, charity organisations and many others for their banking requirements which are financial in nature. The process involves dealing with large number of transactions, accounting processes and data processing. Before the advent of information technology, all these transactions were done manually. It used to be labour‐intensive and time‐ consuming. Inter‐city transactions used to take days if not month(s).

Developments in the field of information and communication technology have altered the banking scenario. The world has become one for all practical purposes in regard to conducting financial transactions amongst entities irrespective of their location. The time as well as effort required in conducting transactions, accounting and generating reports has been drastically reduced. The accuracy and control over bank operations have undergone a sea change.

About three decades back the transformation began. Changes in technology have been fast and comprehensive. The software solutions now cover almost all logically defined banking activities. The secured messaging system has now been standardised to enable seamless secured communication between two different software applications. The hardware has become more rugged, smaller and cheaper. The communication channels have moved from leased line to Very Small Aperture Terminal (VSAT) to mobile communication. The cost of acquisition of technology has reduced substantially. The messaging system is now becoming more and more secured, moving towards finger/eye/face recognition. The networking technologies have further brought everyone on to a single platform. The mobile communication system has enhanced the convenience to do transactions while being on the move.

Technology driven ‘Smart Banking’ is the backbone of today’s successful banks. While it is necessary to follow the fundamentals of sound banking practices, it is of paramount importance for banks to switch over to ‘Smart Banking’ to be able to remain in competition. The latest innovations in Information and Communication Technology need to be leveraged to create new products and services. The Technology today is no longer only an enabler of banking, but it drives banking, creating opportunities to develop more products and services. ‘Smart Banking’ is a win‐win situation for both, the bank and its customers. While it provides convenience of anytime anywhere online banking to customers, it enables banks to have operational control, efficiency and critical MIS.

Banks have inducted technology in almost every vertical of banking. This has enabled the banks to offer more and more facilities which had never been envisaged before. This is an ongoing process with more and more innovative applications being employed every day. Key technology driven banking products, services and solutions are listed below:

  • Any Branch Banking i.e. Core Banking Solution (CBS)
  • Anywhere Anytime Banking ‐
    • Automated Teller Machine (ATM)
    • Point of Sale Terminals (PoS)
    • Internet Banking
    • Mobile Banking
  • MICR Clearing
  • Cheque Truncation System (CTS)
  • Instant Funds Transfer ‐
    • National Electronic Funds Transfer (NEFT)
    • Real Time Gross Settlement (RTGS)
    • Immediate Payment Service (IMPS)
  • Electronic Clearing Service (ECS)
  • National Automatic Clearing House (NACH)
  • Online Payment for e‐Commerce Transactions
  • Online Settlement of Stock Market Transactions
  • Aadhaar Enabled Services ‐
    • Aadhaar Payment Bridge System
    • Aadhaar Enabled Payment System
    • Aadhaar Based Account Opening
  • Auto Sweep in/out and Flexi Fixed Deposit Facilities
  • Wealth Management Service
  • Online Services for International Trade and Payments
  • Cash Management Service (CMS)
  • Customer Relationship Management (CRM)
  • Asset Liability Management (ALM)
  • Anti‐Money Laundering (AML)
  • Treasury Systems
  • International Electronic Financial Communication Network
  • Structured Financial Management System (SFMS)

While Core Banking Solution is the main software application for banking operations, several other IT solutions, applications and devices are required to drive various products and services mentioned above. For example, different electronic service delivery channels like Internet Banking, ATM, PoS, Micro‐ATM and Mobile Banking operate on independent software solutions. These software solutions are connected to CBS through different communication channels like cable, mobile, internet and VSAT etc. to facilitate online transactions in real‐time environment.

Similarly, there are several activities which, though a part of banking, are outside the operating domain of CBS e.g., Treasury, Asset Liability Management, Anti‐Money Laundering system and Customer Relationship Management etc. To carry out these activities, banks require Software Solutions. These solutions have their own independent logic, systems and processes. These software solutions are independently hosted and are connected to the CBS. Only the transactions relating to bank accounts, generated from these solutions, are reflected in the CBS.

In addition, there are activities which are not essential core banking activities but still form an important part of overall bouquet of services of the banks. For example Cash Management System, Wealth Management, Demat Services, Management of Fixed Assets like land/building/furniture, Human Resource Management and Share Capital Management etc. In order to undertake these activities, banks acquire separate software solutions which are integrated with the CBS.

Besides, banks are required to connect with the outside financial payment systems for inter‐bank financial transactions like Card Payment Network, RTGS, NEFT, IMPS and SWIFT etc. For this, they install customised software solutions which are connected to CBS on one hand and to the external payment systems on the other to enable seamless processing of transactions between CBS and the external systems.

CBS is at the centre of entire banking operations. All other systems converge into it. The integration of CBS with other software solutions is through standardised Secured Financial Transaction Messaging System and Application Programming Interface. However, each software application requires customisation for integrating with the CBS structure for seamless processing of transactions. While the aforementioned supplemental systems contain domain specific data, CBS provides up‐to‐date information about ongoing banking operations in the books of accounts of the bank.

This book has been divided into seven parts. First three parts contain details of core retail banking operations. Fourth part gives overview of treasury, securities and foreign exchange trading systems whereas fifth part provides information about other key systems and solutions. Sixth part consists of introduction to financial communication networks which are the backbone of entire electronic banking system. In the last and seventh part of the book authors talk about the key initiatives of the Reserve Bank of India and the way forward.

FOREWORD

The onset of new age private sector banks in the early 1990s paved the way for large scale changes in the Indian banking sector. These banks incorporated the latest technological gadgets for banking operations which resulted in speedier and more reliable transactions. This was a win-win situation both for the banks as well as for their customers. Technological upgradations were done, slowly albeit surely, by public sector banks and the old age private sector banks as well. The smarter banks seized the opportunity and moved miles ahead of others on the back of innovative technology driven products and services. These developments gave birth to a new term called “smart banking” to differentiate technology led banking from traditional way of doing the business of banking.

Since in “smart banking” both technology and banking are closely intertwined, it is a challenge to keep abreast of the latest in the banking technology. The latest would include big data, cloud computing, smart phones, use of home video-conferencing system for personalized connect and other such innovations.

From the banker’s perspective, better technology also leads to cost cutting. As per World Bank estimates, the operational cost per transaction for Indian Banks is Rs. 48 per branch, Rs. 25 for phone banking, Rs. 18 for ATM, Rs. 8 for IVR (Interactive Voice Response) and Rs. 4 for online transaction. Who would have thought some years back that banking transactions could be done hands on through mobile. The annual report of Reserve Bank of India states that the average value of transactions via mobile phones in 2014-15 has beaten the average value of transactions in credit and debit cards. It appears that mobile banking and mobile payments & commerce are truly the future.

One of the most difficult things in the world is to make things simple. This book brings out various aspects of banking and the technology associated with them in simple terms. The book has been written in easy to understand language even for non-bankers. Bankers may find many areas of learning outside their current domain of activity. It may also be useful to persons engaged in banking software sector.

The authors of this book are men of letters and high learning. I feel happy and honoured to introduce this book. I am sure it will scale up the knowledge of the readers with regard to technology driven banking and would also give impetus to bankers to be one step ahead of each other for further technological upgradations.

Dr.Rajnish Kataria
Director
National Institute Of Banking Studies & Corporate Management (NIBSCOM)

PREFACE

The emergence of new technologies and expectations of tech‐savvy customers has brought about a paradigm shift in the lifestyle of people across the world. E‐commerce has altered the shopping style. The speed and accuracy of doing things has dramatically accelerated.

Banking is no exception and globally, every aspect of banking has undergone a sea change. Indian banks have also not been found wanting and have geared up to adapt and absorb technological innovations and solutions in banking.

While earlier, technology was only an enabler, it is now seen as the driver of banking services and banking business and the future seems even more challenging and exciting.

It is no longer sufficient to be an efficient bank. One has to be a ‘smart’ bank offering state‐of‐the‐art products and services.

The rapid strides made in technology due to faster, wider and cheaper communication network; miniaturization, ruggedness and durability of hardware and storage devices; larger and faster CPU capacities; standardised secured messaging between different software applications; development of newer delivery channel devices and development of core banking solutions along with various other banking software products and services gave rise and provided impetus to many new products and services which were truly beyond imagination.

In India, acceptance of new technology in banking was delayed a little, essentially, due to the resistance of the workforce which feared loss of jobs. The steps taken to open up the economy in the early nineties witnessed the entry of new generation private banks. These banks started with the latest technology available and soon captured the imagination of the people. The rapid inroads these banks made into the banking business showcased the power of technology. Very soon public sector banks followed suit. Today, there is hardly any discipline in banking which is untouched by technology.

‘Any Branch Banking’, ‘Anywhere Banking’, ‘Anytime Banking’, ‘Banking from Home’ and ‘Banking on the Move ‘have all eased banking transactions for the people who need not visit their banks for doing business. The technology has taken banking to those areas where no bank had ever reached. With the help of technology, leakages in dispensation of relief to the poor are being plugged through direct credit of money in the accounts of the beneficiaries. Money laundering is being checked through tracking transactions online with the help of technology.

Technology has enabled banks not only to improve their operational efficiency but also has brought about new products and services. Transaction banking is almost entirely automated. Customer Relation Management and Data Analytics are being used to enhance understanding of the business and customer base. Software solutions have been developed for auto‐processing and monitoring of loans. Digitisation of money is reducing the need for physical cash. Paper based payment system is fast migrating into electronic mode.

The need for physical cash has now been reduced to petty payments. Not very distant is the prospect of doing away with the cash for most of our day to day requirements. This will result in the aggregation of this category of capital which is currently lying in the pockets of individuals. It will not only provide security to the people but will also create large financial resources for economic development.

Banking education in general revolves around knowledge about banking and sometimes in specialised technology domains. The knowledge about technology application in banking is generally limited to the people in their respective domains of banking and technology. This book is an attempt to create common understanding amongst students, customers and bankers in general.

The book is not a comprehensive compendium of banking technologies but is aimed at familiarising the readers with the main technology applications in Indian banks.
